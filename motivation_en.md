# Motivation
There are a lot of articles and other sources on programming and its principles. This is especially true for object-oriented (OOP) and procedural approaches to writing a software. And these works, which I think don't need their introduction here, have many interesting examples, both artificial and real-life, that perfectly demonstrate different approaches to system design and writing high-quality code.

There are other sources that describe more mental models, such as that there is no silver bullet or that the system must be error-resistant.

And there are sources that describe how to design any system, whether related to IT or not.

But I have never come across a work that describes from the very bottom to the top the path of a developer in studying programming principles, which, on the one hand, is **abstract** in order to understand that these principles can be used not only in OOP or programming at all, but also in other areas of life where they got their roots long time ago, and on the other — has **examples from different fields**. And in this "bible" I will try to give exactly such **"fundamental" material**.

Why do I see a need for such work?
1. In any field, a complete understanding of the "foundation" allows you to use your knowledge in **non-standard situations** which are more and more crucial especially with the increasing rise of AI usage.
2. Analogies make it possible to understand any knowledge **much more effectively** as they build on top of common knowledge or interesting facts that are easy to remember.
3. It helps in **developing the skill of finding analogies** — viewing a large number of analogies can help in developing such a habit which is one of the best ways to solve problems.

It's worth mentioning that I do not recognize SOLID. That is not because I believe that these principles do not work, on the contrary, they lead to high—quality code. I dismiss the presentation of SOLID as the five testaments that you must memorize as an excerpt from the Old Testament, page 59 (random page number). And the fact that it is served with examples from the OOP, in my opinion, does not help much in the situation. **SOLID should be presented as a result of knowledge on general system design**, which is fundamental and will work not only in OOP, like SOLID, but also in functional programming and in designing the structure of a large business. And in this "bible" I will focus on these **fundamentals**, and then separately show how they lead to concepts familiar to everyone in software engineering.

If such a work already exists, then at least I will put what I know on the shelves in my head, and maybe I will help readers understand some areas of programming better than they're described in an existing similar work.
